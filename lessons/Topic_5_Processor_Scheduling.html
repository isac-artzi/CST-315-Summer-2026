Where this topic sits in the factory

Topic 5 is the floor supervisor's job description. Many work orders are queued and only a few stations are free. The supervisor decides whose order goes on which station, how long they stay, and what to do when something urgent comes in. That is scheduling.

The supervisor uses a policy. Different policies produce different fairness, throughput, and responsiveness. Project 5 has you build your own supervisor — design the policy, defend the choice, expose hooks so an outside command (your shell) can tune it.

1What you're optimizing for

Schedulers are evaluated against a small set of metrics, and any choice is a trade-off. CPU utilization: keep cores busy. Throughput: processes completed per unit time. Turnaround time: completion time minus arrival time. Waiting time: time in the ready queue. Response time: time from arrival to first running. Fairness: comparable processes get comparable service.

Interactive workloads care most about response time. Batch workloads care about throughput and turnaround. A single scheduler cannot perfectly serve both; modern systems either pick a sweet spot or maintain multiple queues with different policies.

Factory translation. Some customers wait at the counter (response time matters). Some submit big overnight jobs (throughput matters). The supervisor balances them. Pick the wrong policy and you'll either have idle stations or a long line of irritated customers.

2The classical algorithms

First-Come, First-Served (FCFS): ready queue is FIFO. Simple, fair-ish, but a long process at the front penalizes everyone behind it (convoy effect). Shortest Job First (SJF): pick the process with the shortest expected burst. Optimal for average wait time — if you know the burst length, which you usually don't. Shortest Remaining Time (SRT): preemptive SJF; new arrivals can kick out longer-remaining current processes.

Round Robin (RR): each process gets a time slice (quantum); when the quantum expires, it goes to the back of the queue. Excellent response time; turnaround scales with quantum choice. Too short: context-switch overhead dominates. Too long: degenerates to FCFS. Priority scheduling: each process has a priority; highest priority runs first. Risk: starvation of low-priority processes. Mitigation: aging — increase priority the longer a process waits.

Multilevel Feedback Queue (MLFQ): multiple ready queues at different priorities, processes demoted if they use their full quantum (CPU-bound) and promoted if they release early (I/O-bound). Approximates SJF without knowing burst lengths. Variants run the world's interactive systems.

3Modern Linux scheduling — CFS and beyond

Linux uses the Completely Fair Scheduler (CFS) for most processes. CFS abandons fixed time slices and runs the process with the smallest virtual runtime (vruntime) so far. Each process's vruntime increases at a rate inversely proportional to its weight (priority/nice value). The result: all processes make smooth progress, weighted by priority, without explicit queues or quantums.

Real-time processes use separate classes (SCHED_FIFO, SCHED_RR) that bypass CFS. As of 2023, Linux is also shipping EEVDF (Earliest Eligible Virtual Deadline First) as a successor scheduler that combines fairness with explicit latency guarantees. Project 5 doesn't require you to reimplement CFS, but you should be able to discuss why a real scheduler does not look like textbook RR.

Modern callout. When you read about Kubernetes "CPU shares" or container "CPU weights" — that's CFS's weight parameter, exposed through cgroups. The same scheduler that picks which terminal gets your keystrokes also distributes cloud-scale compute.
Factory translation. CFS is the supervisor with a stopwatch. They don't say "you get 10 minutes then I rotate." They say "you've had the least running-time so far; you're next." High-weight processes accrue runtime more slowly, so they get more wall clock.

4Implementation considerations for your scheduler

Project 5's spec lists 11 questions you must answer in design and code. The big ones: How is the queue represented? (linked list, array, heap, multilevel structure?). Is it preemptive? (Round Robin yes; FCFS no). How do you handle I/O waits? (move to wait queue, mark blocked, requeue on completion). How do you avoid starvation? (aging). How do you minimize overhead? (avoid copying clipboards; keep queue operations O(1) or O(log n)).

The other dimension is the user interface: implement `procs` to list processes and states, with `-a` for detail and `-si` for sorting. Implement a command to modify priority — and expose that command as a system call so external tools can change scheduling parameters at runtime. That's objective 4 ("modify a parameter via a system call").

Check for understanding

  1. Compute average waiting time for FCFS on the workload {P1: arrival 0 burst 7, P2: arrival 2 burst 4, P3: arrival 4 burst 1}.
    Show a sample answer P1 starts at 0, finishes at 7 (wait 0). P2 arrives at 2, runs at 7, finishes at 11 (wait 5). P3 arrives at 4, runs at 11, finishes at 12 (wait 7). Avg = (0+5+7)/3 = 4. Now do the same workload under SJF and see the convoy effect disappear.
  2. If your RR quantum is huge (say larger than every burst), what does RR degrade to?
    Show a sample answer FCFS. No process gets preempted, so the queue is processed in arrival order.
  3. Why is starvation a risk in priority scheduling and not in RR?
    Show a sample answer Priority always picks the highest-priority ready process; a low-priority one can wait indefinitely if higher-priority work keeps arriving. RR rotates through all processes in the queue regardless of priority, so every process eventually gets a turn.
  4. Your scheduler receives 1000 ready processes. The queue is a sorted linked list. Each scheduling decision is O(n) just to find the head. Why is that a problem and what's the standard fix?
    Show a sample answer At 1000 processes, that's a long walk per decision, and you make a decision on every preemption. Standard fixes: a min-heap (O(log n) insert and find-min), or maintain a per-priority array of FIFOs (O(1) find-min), or — like CFS — a red-black tree keyed on vruntime (O(log n) but better constants and self-balancing).
